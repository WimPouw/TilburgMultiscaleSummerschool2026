Part 1

MaskAnyone

Full-stack de-identification with YOLO + SAM2

MaskAnyone architecture

How it works

  1. Upload — video or folder via UI or API
  2. Detect — YOLOv8-pose finds all people per frame
  3. Segment — SAM2 creates pixel-accurate masks
  4. Apply strategy — blur / pixelate / solid / contours
  5. Export — masked MP4 + pose JSON
GPU required for SAM2. CPU fallback available with MediaPipe segmenter.

Quick start

# 1. Start server (one-time setup)
git clone https://github.com/MaskAnyone/MaskAnyone
cd MaskAnyone
docker compose up -d

# 2. Open UI
open http://localhost:5001

# 3. Or use the API
curl -X POST http://localhost:8080/videos \
  -F "file=@clip.mp4"

First pull downloads ~8 GB model weights.

The Privacy–Utility Dilemma

How much signal do we lose when we protect identities?

Babajide Owoyele  ·  Radboud University / SYNAPSIS
15:15 – 16:00

The core tension

Privacy

  • GDPR Article 4(1) — biometric data is sensitive personal data
  • Face recognition trivially re-identifies blurred video at scale (Gross et al. 2006)
  • IRB / ethics committees increasingly require de-identification before sharing
  • Participants have a right to withdraw — data must be revisable
Face & voice are directly identifying. Background details, gait, and body shape can also re-identify.

Scientific utility

  • Body movement data requires visible joints
  • Gesture research needs hand and head motion
  • Vocal analysis requires original audio — or its acoustic properties
  • Heavy masking → degraded signal, reduced replicability
MaskBench shows body keypoints (RMSE < 10 px) survive most masking strategies.

What masking cannot hide

Residual identifiers

  • Gait — body sway, step pattern are person-specific (Nixon et al. 2006)
  • Hand shape — even silhouette reveals knuckle width
  • Clothing — colour, pattern, logos visible through partial blur
  • Voice — even pitch-shifted audio retains prosodic signature
  • Background — room, posters, unique objects

Mitigations

  • Full solid fill + skeleton removes most residuals
  • Audio replacement (TTS) eliminates voice entirely
  • Background replacement — chroma key or inpainting
  • Access control — tiered release; IRB-only for raw
  • Time jitter — shift session timestamps to prevent linking
No de-identification method is 100% proof against a determined adversary with auxiliary information.

Legal & ethical framing

GDPR requirements

  • Art. 4(1) — identified or identifiable natural person
  • Art. 9 — biometric & health data as special category
  • Art. 89 — research exemption, but requires safeguards
  • Recital 26 — pseudonymisation ≠ anonymisation if re-linkable
Video is personal data under GDPR by default. Consent ≠ free access to identifiable recordings.

Ethics committee checklist

  • Required Informed consent covering recording
  • Required Data management plan
  • Required Storage location (EU/EEA preferred)
  • Recommended De-identification before sharing
  • Recommended Access-controlled repository (DANS, OSF private)
  • Optional Right-to-erasure workflow for each participant

Discussion

Where do we draw the line?

Group exercise — 10 minutes

Discussion scenarios

Scenario A

You have 30-minute design sessions with three participants per group. You want to share 5-minute excerpts as open-access supplementary material. What strategy do you choose?

Scenario B

A participant withdraws consent two years after recording. The masked video is already in a repository with a DOI. How do you handle the withdrawal?

Scenario C

A reviewer requests access to raw (unmasked) video to verify annotation quality. What access control mechanism do you put in place?

There is no universally correct answer. The right choice depends on your data sensitivity, participant consent scope, and intended audience.

A practical decision framework

AudienceVideo accessAudio access Recommended strategy
Research team only Raw Raw Secure institutional storage; no sharing
Restricted collaborators Blur Original + NDA DANS Data Station SSH / OSF private
Peer review Solid fill Pitch-shifted Embargoed DOI; reviewer access link
Open access Silhouette TTS replacement DataverseNL / Zenodo public
The Envision triadic dataset uses solid fill + skeleton overlay for internal sharing and skeleton-only export for open access.

Key takeaways

  • De-identification ≠ anonymisation — GDPR still applies unless re-linkage is truly impossible
  • No one-size-fits-all — match strategy to audience and data sensitivity
  • Body keypoints survive masking — MaskBench shows PCK > 88% for wrists/shoulders
  • Audio is often the weakest link — pitch shift alone is insufficient; consider TTS or suppression
  • Design for layered access — keep raw data under strict control, release progressively masked versions

Masking Taxonomy

Reference · Thursday · Tilburg Multiscale Summerschool 2026

A Taxonomy of Video Masking

Organising de-identification methods by mechanism, modality, and reversibility

Babajide Owoyele  ·  Radboud University / SYNAPSIS
Reference slide deck · Thursday Masking Day

Why organise by taxonomy?

The literature uses blur, anonymisation, de-identification, and obfuscation interchangeably — and often imprecisely.

A taxonomy lets us:

  • Choose the right method for a given threat model
  • Compare methods in MaskBench on equal terms
  • Communicate clearly to ethics boards
  • Design reproducible de-identification pipelines
We distinguish three orthogonal axes: mechanism (how), modality (what), and reversibility (can it be undone?).
Irreversible ≠ anonymised: gait and body proportions are stable identifiers that no pixel-level method removes.

Axis 1 — Mechanism

How is the transformation applied?

Signal degradation — reduce information content in pixel space
Spatial blur — Gaussian, box, motion blur
Spatial quantisation — pixelation, mosaic
Noise injection — Gaussian, salt-and-pepper
Region replacement — substitute person region
Solid fill — colour constant, configurable
Inpainting — generative background fill (LaMa, Stable Diffusion)
Silhouette / contour — shape outline only
Representation replacement — replace with abstract signal
Skeleton overlay — stick figure from keypoints only
Pose re-rendering — neural avatar driven by pose (NeRF, UV-maps)
Face swapping — synthetic face replacement (DeepFaceLab)

Axis 2 — Modality

What signal is being de-identified?

Visual
Face — primary identity signal
Whole body — full person region
Hands — fingerprints, ring patterns
Background — location, posters, labels
Screen content — slides, documents in frame
Audio
Voice — pitch, timbre, accent
Paralanguage — laughter, breathing, prosody
Background audio — identifiable sounds
Metadata
Timestamps — date, time, session order
Filename / path — participant ID in filename

Axis 3 — Reversibility

Irreversible (destructive)

  • Blur, pixelate, solid fill — pixel information destroyed
  • Audio deletion — silence or noise
  • Face inpainting — background painted over
Once destroyed, original data is unrecoverable — keep raw master under strict access control.

Reversible (with key)

  • Encryption-based — encrypted region, decryptable by authorised party
  • Pseudonymisation — participant code table held separately
  • Alpha-channel masking — original stored as separate layer
Reversible masking enables withdrawal compliance — participant data can be fully erased post-deposit.

Audio de-identification methods

Signal processing

Low compute

  • Pitch shifting (librosa)
  • Noise injection
  • Formant scrambling
  • Time stretching
  • Low-pass filtering

Moderate privacy

Preserves prosody and timing. Speaker style partially retained.

Voice conversion

Medium compute

  • GAN-based VC (StarGAN-VC)
  • VITS / YourTTS
  • RVC (Real-time VC)
  • Emotion-preserving VC

High privacy

Converts to a different speaker voice. May preserve linguistic content.

Speech re-synthesis

High compute

  • Whisper ASR → text
  • pyttsx3 / Coqui TTS
  • Bark / Tortoise TTS
  • Pyannote diarization

Highest privacy

Completely new voice. Content preserved, not speaker identity.

We cover methods 1 and 3 in 04_audio_deidentification.ipynb. Voice conversion requires dedicated setup (e.g. RVC inference server).

Choosing the right method

Decision questions

  1. Who will access the data? (team / collaborators / public)
  2. What analysis do you plan? (gesture / gaze / speech)
  3. Do you need audio? (linguistic content vs. identity)
  4. What is the re-identification risk? (clinical vs. students)
  5. Do you have GPU compute? (SAM2 vs. MediaPipe)

Audio De-identification

16:15–16:45 · Thursday Masking Day · Tilburg 2026

Audio Masking &
Multi-Modal Considerations

Voice is biometric data. Masking the video is not enough.

Babajide Owoyele · Radboud University / SYNAPSIS

Why isn't masking the video enough?

  • Voice is biometric — speaker identification works with < 5 seconds of audio
  • Existing tools — Microsoft Azure, Google Speech, AWS Transcribe all have speaker ID APIs
  • GDPR, Art. 9 — voice recordings are special-category biometric data
  • Ethics boards increasingly require both video and audio anonymisation
A masked video with an unmodified audio track is not anonymised — the audio alone re-identifies the speaker.
Even removing the audio track doesn't fully anonymise — lip movements and silent gestures can sometimes decode speech.

Three de-identification strategies

1. Pitch shifting

Raise or lower F0 by ±4 semitones. Fast, reversible. Prosody largely preserved.


Privacy: low–med

2. Noise + formant

Add calibrated noise, shift formants via resample. Confuses speaker ID models.


Privacy: medium

3. Speech → Text → Speech

Transcribe with Whisper, re-synthesise with TTS. Original voice is gone entirely.


Privacy: high
Open notebooks/04_audio_deidentification.ipynb to run all three methods on your own audio.

The privacy–utility trade-off in audio

Method Speaker ID blocked? Pitch preserved? Prosody preserved? Intelligible?
Pitch shift Partial No (shifted) Yes Yes
Noise + formant Mostly Approx. Partial Yes
S → T → S Yes No No Yes (content)
Strip audio Yes N/A N/A No audio

Multi-speaker sessions: diarise first

The Envision triadic sessions have 3 speakers. Apply a different anonymisation per speaker so they remain distinguishable.


# pyannote speaker diarization → per-speaker pitch shifts
pipeline = Pipeline.from_pretrained("pyannote/speaker-diarization-3.1")
diarization = pipeline("group_07_cam_p1.wav")

speaker_shifts = {}           # e.g. SPEAKER_00 → +4st, SPEAKER_01 → -4st
shift_pool = [+4, -4, +6]

for turn, _, speaker in diarization.itertracks(yield_label=True):
    if speaker not in speaker_shifts:
        speaker_shifts[speaker] = shift_pool[len(speaker_shifts)]
    # apply pitch_shift() to this segment of audio
Requires a HuggingFace token and accepting pyannote terms — see notebook cell for setup.

Connection to Tuesday acoustic analysis

What Tuesday measured

  • Fundamental frequency (F0) — pitch
  • Intensity / energy
  • Formant frequencies (F1, F2)
  • Speech rate, pauses

What de-id destroys

  • Pitch shift — F0 values are invalid
  • Noise injection — intensity and formants distorted
  • S → T → S — all prosodic detail lost
Best practice: extract and archive all acoustic features you need before de-identifying the audio.

Quick reference: audio operations with ffmpeg 


# Extract audio track as WAV (for analysis or de-id)
ffmpeg -i session.mp4 -vn -acodec pcm_s16le audio.wav

# Strip audio entirely (for open-tier archive)
ffmpeg -i masked.mp4 -an -c:v copy masked_silent.mp4

# Replace audio track with de-identified version
ffmpeg -i masked_video.mp4 -i deid_audio.wav \
  -map 0:v -map 1:a -c:v copy -c:a aac \
  masked_with_deid_audio.mp4

# Apply pitch shift via ffmpeg's rubberband filter (no Python needed)
ffmpeg -i audio.wav -af "rubberband=pitch=1.26" shifted.wav

pitch 1.26 ≈ +4 semitones   pitch 0.79 ≈ −4 semitones
